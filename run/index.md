# Recommended Program

This program is organized into four workout categories — easy days, threshold days, interval days, and long run days — and follows an 80/20 intensity distribution: roughly 80% of weekly volume at easy effort, 20% at moderate-to-hard effort. Within each category, workouts are listed in order of priority and frequency. Not every workout needs to appear every week — select based on training phase, recovery status, and race goals. A typical week includes 3 easy days, 1 threshold day, 1 interval day, and 1 long run day, totaling 5–6 runs and 40–55 miles per week.

This program is tailored for a 1:39 half marathoner (5'8", 160 lbs) with a long torso and large calves. At 160 lbs, impact forces per stride are higher than for lighter runners at this performance level, which means easy days must be genuinely easy to manage cumulative load, and softer surfaces are preferred for long runs. The long torso raises the center of gravity and increases rotational demand on the core during running — arm carriage discipline and trunk stability matter more than for shorter-torso runners. Large calves increase the metabolic cost of leg swing (heavier distal mass costs more energy per stride) but also mean a larger calf-Achilles complex that stores and returns more elastic energy per push-off, benefiting hill running and kick speed. Cadence should trend toward 175–180 spm to offset the swing-cost penalty of heavy calves. Monitor Achilles loading carefully — more calf muscle generates more force through the tendon.

Pace zones are derived from the 1:39 half marathon (7:33/mi race pace) using standard physiological relationships. Equivalent race fitness: approximately 19:30 5K, 40:30 10K, 3:35 marathon.

| Zone | Pace | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Easy | 8:45–9:30/mi | Base building, recovery |
| Marathon | 7:50–8:00/mi | Goal marathon pace |
| Threshold | 7:15–7:25/mi | Lactate threshold |
| VO2max | 6:15–6:25/mi | Aerobic ceiling (5K effort) |
| Speed | 5:45–5:55/mi | Economy, neuromuscular (mile effort) |
| Repetition | 5:30–5:40/mi | Pure speed (800m effort) |

## Easy Days

Easy days make up the majority of weekly volume and serve as the foundation of the program. They build [aerobic capacity](#aerobic-system) — capillary density, mitochondrial volume, fat oxidation — without accumulating meaningful fatigue. At 160 lbs with large calves, easy pace must be genuinely easy (8:45–9:30/mi, conversational) to protect the [Achilles tendon](#calf-achilles-complex) and manage cumulative impact forces. Running easy days too fast is the single most common training error at this level and the fastest path to overtraining or injury.

**Recovery** ([Aerobic System](#aerobic-system))
1. [Recovery Run](#recovery-run) (active recovery)

The day after a hard session. 3–5 miles at 9:00–9:30/mi or slower — pace should feel trivially easy. The purpose is blood flow for recovery, not fitness. If anything hurts or feels heavy, walk instead. At 160 lbs, the [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost) of even an easy run is higher than for lighter runners, so genuine recovery pace is non-negotiable.

**Base Building** ([Aerobic System](#aerobic-system))
1. [General Aerobic Run](#general-aerobic-run) (base building)
2. [Easy Run + Strides](#easy-run--strides) (base + neuromuscular)

General aerobic runs are the workhorse of the program: 6–9 miles at 8:45–9:15/mi. They develop the [aerobic system](#aerobic-system) across every adaptation — cardiac output, capillary density, mitochondrial density, fat oxidation — with low [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost). Easy runs with strides add 4–6 x 20-second accelerations after the run to maintain neuromuscular speed and [running economy](#running-economy) without adding training stress. Strides are not intervals — full recovery between each, no metabolic fatigue. Schedule strides 2–3 times per week, ideally on days before quality sessions, to prime the nervous system.

## Threshold Days

The [lactate threshold](#lactate-threshold) is the single strongest predictor of half marathon performance. A 1:39 half marathon is run at or just below threshold pace for 99 minutes — improving threshold pace directly lowers race time. Threshold work should appear once per week during base building and can increase to twice per week during the build phase (replacing one easy day), but never two consecutive days.

**Threshold Work** ([Lactate Threshold](#lactate-threshold))
1. [Tempo Run](#tempo-run) (sustained threshold)
2. [Cruise Intervals](#cruise-intervals) (threshold with recovery)
3. [Progression Run](#progression-run) (aerobic → threshold)

Tempo runs are the primary threshold workout: 20–40 minutes of sustained running at 7:15–7:25/mi. They build the ability to hold threshold pace for extended periods, which is the exact demand of a half marathon. Cruise intervals break threshold pace into segments (e.g., 4 x 8 min at threshold with 90 sec jog) to allow more total time at threshold with less [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost) — use these when tempo runs feel too demanding or when accumulating more threshold volume is the goal. Progression runs start easy and finish the last 2–3 miles at threshold pace, teaching the body to run fast on tired legs — this simulates the last 5K of a half marathon and develops the mental discipline to push through fatigue. [Race-pace intervals](#race-pace-intervals) are an alternative when half-marathon-specific pace practice is needed, but tempo runs and cruise intervals provide more threshold stimulus per session.

## Interval Days

[VO2max](#vo2max) sets the ceiling for all aerobic performance — you cannot race faster than your aerobic ceiling allows. Interval work raises this ceiling and improves [running economy](#running-economy), making every pace feel easier. One interval session per week is sufficient for most training phases; increase to two only during peak phases and only if recovery allows.

**Interval Work** ([VO2max](#vo2max), [Running Economy](#running-economy))
1. [VO2max Intervals](#vo2max-intervals) (aerobic ceiling)
2. [Hill Repeats](#hill-repeats) (power + economy)
3. [Speed Intervals](#speed-intervals) (neuromuscular / economy)

VO2max intervals are the priority: 4–6 x 1000m–1200m at 6:15–6:25/mi (5K effort) with equal-duration jog recovery. This is the workout that spends the most time at maximal oxygen uptake and produces the largest [VO2max](#vo2max) gains per session. Hill repeats follow as a high-priority alternative: 8–12 x 60–90 seconds hard uphill with jog-down recovery. Hills build specific strength with lower impact forces than flat speed work — critical at 160 lbs where [injury risk](#injury-risk) from high-speed flat running is elevated. For large calves, hills heavily load the [calf-Achilles complex](#calf-achilles-complex) concentrically (push-off power) with less eccentric stress than downhill running. For the long torso, uphill running naturally increases forward lean, which is mechanically favorable. Speed intervals (200–400m at 5:45–5:55/mi) improve [running economy](#running-economy) and fast-twitch recruitment but carry higher [injury risk](#injury-risk) and should be used sparingly — once every 2–3 weeks is sufficient. [Fartlek](#fartlek) is a useful unstructured alternative to formal intervals when mental freshness matters or when track access is limited.

## Long Run Days

The long run is the cornerstone of half marathon training. It builds time on feet, [fat oxidation](#aerobic-system), glycogen storage, musculoskeletal durability, and mental toughness. At 160 lbs, long runs generate more cumulative impact than for lighter runners, so cap distance at 14–16 miles and use softer surfaces (trails, grass, packed dirt) when available. One long run per week, always followed by an easy or rest day.

**Long Runs** ([Aerobic System](#aerobic-system), [Lactate Threshold](#lactate-threshold))
1. [Steady Long Run](#steady-long-run) (aerobic endurance)
2. [Progressive Long Run](#progressive-long-run) (aerobic → marathon pace)
3. [Long Run with Threshold Segments](#long-run-with-threshold-segments) (race-specific)

Steady long runs are the default: 12–16 miles at easy pace (8:45–9:15/mi). They build the aerobic foundation that every other workout depends on. Progressive long runs start at easy pace and finish the last 3–4 miles at [marathon pace](#recommended-program) (7:50–8:00/mi), teaching the body to run faster on fatigued legs while managing [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost) — they simulate the back half of a race without the full recovery cost. Long runs with threshold segments insert 2–3 x 10–15 min at [threshold pace](#tempo-run) into the middle or end of the long run, providing race-specific stimulus at the highest [specificity](#specificity) of any training session. Use steady long runs during base building, progressive long runs during build phase, and segmented long runs during peak phase as your fitness sharpens.

# Running Workouts

## Easy Workouts

### Recovery Run

Run 3–5 miles at 9:00–9:30/mi or slower, keeping heart rate below 65% of max. The purpose is active recovery, not fitness — blood flow accelerates muscle repair and glycogen replenishment without adding training stress. Should feel effortless from the first step; if your legs feel heavy, shorten the run or walk. At 160 lbs, even easy running generates substantial ground reaction forces (approximately 2.5x body weight per stride), so genuine recovery pace protects the [musculoskeletal system](#musculoskeletal) from cumulative overload. Schedule after every hard session. Choose this over a rest day when you feel recovered enough to move but not enough to train; choose a rest day when genuine fatigue, soreness, or sleep debt is present.

### General Aerobic Run

Run 6–9 miles at 8:45–9:15/mi, keeping heart rate in the 65–75% zone. The bread and butter of distance training: develops every component of the [aerobic system](#aerobic-system) — capillary density, mitochondrial volume, fat oxidation, cardiac stroke volume — with low [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost). Effort should be conversational (you can speak in full sentences). Run 3–4 of these per week. The temptation is to run these too fast — at 160 lbs, the aerobic benefit of running 8:30 versus 9:00 is negligible, but the impact cost is not. Choose this over a [recovery run](#recovery-run) when feeling fresh and capable; choose a recovery run when legs are heavy from a previous hard session.

### Easy Run + Strides

Run 5–7 miles at easy pace, then perform 4–6 x 20-second strides (smooth accelerations to ~90% sprint, with full recovery walk/jog between each). [Strides](#strides) maintain neuromuscular speed, fast-twitch fiber recruitment, and [running economy](#running-economy) without adding metabolic stress. They are not sprints and should not leave you out of breath. The combination primes the nervous system for quality work the following day. With large calves, strides are an efficient way to train fast-twitch fibers without the [injury risk](#injury-risk) of full interval sessions. Schedule 2–3 times per week, ideally the day before a threshold or interval session. Choose this over a plain [general aerobic run](#general-aerobic-run) when you want neuromuscular maintenance; choose the plain aerobic run when simplicity or fatigue management is the priority.

## Threshold Workouts

### Tempo Run

After a 1–2 mile warm-up, run 20–40 minutes continuously at [threshold pace](#lactate-threshold) (7:15–7:25/mi), then 1–2 mile cool-down. This is "comfortably hard" — you can speak in short phrases but not hold a conversation. The sustained effort teaches your body to clear lactate at the rate it is being produced, raising the pace at which this equilibrium occurs. This is the most [specific](#specificity) threshold workout because the half marathon itself is a sustained threshold effort. Build from 20 minutes during base phase to 40 minutes during peak phase. Total session including warm-up and cool-down runs 7–10 miles. Choose this over [cruise intervals](#cruise-intervals) when building sustained threshold tolerance is the priority; choose cruise intervals when fatigue management or higher total threshold volume is needed.

### Cruise Intervals

After a 1–2 mile warm-up, run 4–6 x 5–8 minutes at threshold pace (7:15–7:25/mi) with 60–90 seconds of easy jogging between intervals, then 1–2 mile cool-down. Breaking threshold pace into segments with brief recovery allows more total time at threshold (potentially 30–48 minutes of quality vs. 20–40 continuous) with lower [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost) per minute. The short recovery jogs are just enough to reset but not enough to fully recover — lactate remains elevated throughout. Cruise intervals are the preferred threshold workout when returning from a down week, when threshold fitness is still developing, or when accumulating volume at threshold matters more than sustaining it. Total session runs 8–11 miles. Choose this over a [tempo run](#tempo-run) when more threshold volume at lower fatigue cost is the goal; choose the tempo run when sustained half-marathon simulation is the priority.

### Progression Run

Run 7–10 miles total, starting at easy pace (8:45–9:15/mi) and gradually accelerating over the course of the run so that the final 2–3 miles are at threshold pace (7:15–7:25/mi). The shift should be smooth, not abrupt — think of it as a run that gets slightly faster every mile. Progression runs develop the ability to run fast on tired legs, which is the defining challenge of the second half of a half marathon. They also accumulate easy mileage alongside quality work, making them time-efficient. The physiological stress is lower than a [tempo run](#tempo-run) because less total time is spent at threshold, but the psychological training — holding pace as fatigue builds — is invaluable. Choose this over a [tempo run](#tempo-run) when you want quality with less total fatigue; choose the tempo run when maximizing time at threshold is the goal.

## Interval Workouts

### VO2max Intervals

After a 2-mile warm-up with strides, run 4–6 x 1000m–1200m at VO2max pace (6:15–6:25/mi, 5K effort) with equal-duration jog recovery (e.g., 4 min on / 4 min jog). The interval length and recovery are designed to maximize time at 95–100% of [VO2max](#vo2max), which is the primary stimulus for raising the aerobic ceiling. Intervals shorter than 3 minutes do not allow oxygen consumption to reach VO2max; intervals longer than 5 minutes accumulate excessive lactate that forces a pace slowdown. The equal recovery maintains enough fatigue carryover that subsequent intervals reach VO2max faster. Cool down with 1–2 miles easy. Total session runs 7–9 miles. This is the highest-priority interval workout for half marathon improvement. Run once per week during build and peak phases, every 10–14 days during base phase. Choose this over [speed intervals](#speed-intervals) when raising the aerobic ceiling is the priority; choose speed intervals when [running economy](#running-economy) and neuromuscular speed are the focus.

### Hill Repeats

After a 1–2 mile warm-up, run 8–12 x 60–90 seconds hard uphill (roughly VO2max effort by heart rate, but the hill limits speed) with jog-down recovery. Find a hill with a 4–8% grade. Hills build specific strength in the [glutes](#hip-extensors), [calves](#calf-achilles-complex), and [hip flexors](#hip-extensors) while delivering a VO2max-level cardiovascular stimulus at lower ground speed — which means lower impact forces per stride. This is particularly valuable at 160 lbs, where high-speed flat running increases [injury risk](#injury-risk). Large calves benefit from hill repeats because the concentric push-off demand matches their strength, and the reduced eccentric loading on the downhill jog protects the [Achilles tendon](#calf-achilles-complex). Cool down with 1–2 miles easy. Total session runs 6–8 miles. Choose this over [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) when managing impact forces or building leg strength is the priority; choose VO2max intervals when maximizing time at VO2max matters more.

### Speed Intervals

After a 2-mile warm-up with strides, run 8–12 x 200m–400m at speed pace (5:45–5:55/mi for 400s, slightly faster for 200s) with 200m–400m jog recovery. These are fast and controlled, not all-out sprints. Speed intervals improve [running economy](#running-economy) by teaching the neuromuscular system to produce force more efficiently, recruiting fast-twitch fibers that easy running does not reach, and refining biomechanics at high speed. With large calves, the elastic energy return from the [calf-Achilles complex](#calf-achilles-complex) is most pronounced at high stride rates, making this a productive workout for leveraging that anatomical advantage. However, the [injury risk](#injury-risk) from high-speed running is the highest of any workout — limit to once every 2–3 weeks. Cool down with 1–2 miles easy. Total session runs 6–8 miles. Choose this over [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) when speed, economy, and fast-twitch recruitment are the focus; choose VO2max intervals for aerobic development.

### Fartlek

After a 1-mile warm-up, alternate between surges of 1–5 minutes at threshold-to-VO2max effort and equal or longer periods of easy running, for a total of 6–9 miles. "Fartlek" means "speed play" — the structure is deliberately unstructured. Surge pace, duration, and recovery are adjusted by feel rather than GPS or stopwatch. Fartlek develops the ability to change pace and recover on the fly, which mirrors the demands of racing (surging to cover a move, recovering on a downhill, pushing up a hill). It also provides a mental break from the rigidity of structured intervals. The blended intensity hits both [threshold](#lactate-threshold) and [VO2max](#vo2max) zones without the psychological pressure of hitting exact splits. Choose this over [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) or [cruise intervals](#cruise-intervals) when mental freshness or terrain variety is the priority; choose structured intervals when precise intensity targeting matters.

## Long Run Workouts

### Steady Long Run

Run 12–16 miles at easy pace (8:45–9:15/mi), starting conservatively and holding even effort throughout. The steady long run builds aerobic endurance, fat oxidation efficiency, glycogen storage capacity, and musculoskeletal durability — the time on feet is the stimulus, not the pace. At 160 lbs, each mile generates roughly 1,500 foot strikes at 2.5x body weight, so the cumulative load of a 14-mile run is approximately 5.3 million pounds of total force. Use softer surfaces (trails, grass, packed dirt) when available to reduce impact stress. Cap at 16 miles — beyond that, the [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost) and [injury risk](#injury-risk) at this body weight outweigh the marginal aerobic benefit. The steady long run is the default and should make up most long runs throughout the year. Choose this over [progressive long runs](#progressive-long-run) or [segmented long runs](#long-run-with-threshold-segments) during base building or when cumulative fatigue is high; choose the quality long run variants during build and peak phases.

### Progressive Long Run

Run 12–15 miles, starting the first 8–10 miles at easy pace (8:45–9:15/mi) and accelerating the final 3–4 miles to marathon pace (7:50–8:00/mi). The acceleration should be gradual — each mile slightly faster than the last, not an abrupt gear shift. Progressive long runs teach the body to run faster on fatigued legs, build confidence in pace judgment, and simulate the back half of a race. The physiological benefit is time on feet at easy pace (aerobic development) combined with marathon-pace work on pre-fatigued muscles (metabolic and muscular [specificity](#specificity)). The [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost) is higher than a steady long run but lower than a [segmented long run](#long-run-with-threshold-segments), making it a good middle ground. Choose this over a [steady long run](#steady-long-run) when building race-pace confidence on tired legs; choose the steady long run when managing cumulative fatigue.

### Long Run with Threshold Segments

Run 13–16 miles total with 2–3 segments of 10–15 minutes at [threshold pace](#tempo-run) (7:15–7:25/mi) inserted into the middle and/or end of the run, with easy running between segments. Example: 4 miles easy, 12 min at threshold, 3 miles easy, 12 min at threshold, 2 miles easy. This is the most race-specific long run workout because it combines high mileage with sustained quality, simulating the physiological demands of racing a half marathon. The threshold segments on pre-fatigued legs build lactate clearance capacity under conditions that replicate the final miles of a race. The [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost) is the highest of any long run variant and requires 2–3 easy days to recover from. Limit to once every 2–3 weeks during build and peak phases. Choose this over a [progressive long run](#progressive-long-run) when race-specific threshold work is the priority; choose the progressive long run for a less fatiguing quality long run.

## Supplementary Workouts

### Strides

Smooth 20-second accelerations to approximately 90% of maximum sprint speed, performed on flat ground after an easy run. Start from a jog, build to near-top speed over 60–80 meters, then decelerate. Walk or jog 60–90 seconds between each. Strides maintain fast-twitch fiber recruitment, neuromuscular coordination, and [running economy](#running-economy) without adding metabolic training stress — they are not intervals and should not cause heavy breathing. With large calves, strides are an efficient way to train elastic energy return in the [calf-Achilles complex](#calf-achilles-complex) at high stride rates. Do 4–6 strides, 2–3 times per week, typically after easy runs. Choose this over [speed intervals](#speed-intervals) for daily neuromuscular maintenance; choose speed intervals for dedicated economy and fast-twitch development.

### Shakeout Run

Run 2–4 miles at 9:00–9:30/mi, often the day before a race or the day before a key workout. The purpose is to loosen the legs, confirm nothing hurts, and calm pre-race anxiety without accumulating any fatigue. Shorter and easier than a [recovery run](#recovery-run). Often includes 3–4 strides at the end to prime the neuromuscular system. Choose this over a rest day the day before a race when you prefer running to inactivity; choose rest when fatigue or travel makes running impractical.

### Race-Pace Intervals

After a 2-mile warm-up, run 3–5 x 1 mile at goal half marathon pace (7:15–7:25/mi for a sub-1:35 target, or current race pace for maintenance) with 90 seconds jog recovery. These are pace-specific sessions that teach your body and mind what race pace feels like, calibrate internal pacing cues, and build confidence. The pace should feel controlled and sustainable, not hard — if it feels hard, race pace expectations may need adjustment. Total session runs 8–10 miles. Schedule during the final 4–6 weeks before a goal race. Choose this over [tempo runs](#tempo-run) when precise race-pace calibration is the priority; choose tempo runs for broader threshold development.

# Running Physiology

## Energy Systems

Every run engages multiple energy systems simultaneously, but the contribution of each system shifts with intensity and duration. Understanding these systems clarifies why different workouts target different adaptations and why the training mix matters.

### Aerobic System

The primary energy system for all running longer than about 2 minutes. [Mitochondria](#mitochondria) in muscle cells oxidize fat and carbohydrates in the presence of oxygen to produce ATP. The aerobic system is limited by two broad categories: oxygen delivery (cardiac output, hemoglobin, capillary density) and oxygen utilization ([mitochondrial density](#mitochondria), oxidative enzyme activity). Easy running develops this system through every mechanism: increased capillary density brings more blood to working muscles, increased [mitochondrial volume](#mitochondria) processes more oxygen per cell, improved fat oxidation spares glycogen for higher intensities, and increased cardiac stroke volume delivers more oxygen per heartbeat. At 160 lbs, the aerobic system must support a heavier body, which means absolute oxygen consumption is higher at any given pace compared to lighter runners — this is why easy pace should feel genuinely easy, to keep the aerobic system within the fat-oxidation-dominant zone. The aerobic system provides 95–99% of energy during a half marathon.

### Mitochondria

Mitochondria are the organelles inside muscle cells where aerobic energy production occurs — the engines of endurance. Every molecule of ATP produced aerobically is made here, through the electron transport chain on the inner mitochondrial membrane. Endurance performance is determined not just by how much oxygen reaches the muscles but by how much of that oxygen the muscles can actually use, and mitochondrial density is the primary bottleneck on the utilization side.

**What mitochondria do.** Mitochondria oxidize two fuels: fatty acids (via beta-oxidation) and pyruvate from carbohydrate breakdown (via the Krebs cycle). Both pathways converge on the electron transport chain, which uses oxygen as the final electron acceptor to produce ATP. At easy intensities, fat oxidation dominates because it is the preferred pathway when oxygen supply is abundant and energy demand is moderate. As intensity increases toward [threshold](#lactate-threshold), carbohydrate oxidation takes over because glycolysis produces pyruvate faster than mitochondria can process it — the excess pyruvate is converted to lactate. More mitochondria per cell means more capacity to process pyruvate aerobically before the overflow to lactate begins, which is one mechanism by which mitochondrial density raises the lactate threshold.

**How mitochondria adapt.** Training triggers two forms of mitochondrial adaptation: **mitochondrial biogenesis** (creation of new mitochondria) and **mitochondrial remodeling** (improving the function of existing ones). The master regulator of biogenesis is PGC-1-alpha, a transcription coactivator that is upregulated by exercise-induced signals including calcium flux, AMPK activation (triggered by energy depletion), and reactive oxygen species. PGC-1-alpha activates genes that produce new mitochondrial proteins, which are assembled into new mitochondria or fused with existing ones. This process takes 24–72 hours per training bout, which is why consistent daily running is more effective for mitochondrial development than sporadic high-volume sessions.

**What each workout does for mitochondria.** Different training intensities trigger mitochondrial adaptation through different signaling pathways, which is why a mixed training program produces larger gains than any single intensity alone:

- **Easy running** ([general aerobic runs](#general-aerobic-run), [steady long runs](#steady-long-run)): High-volume, low-intensity running maximizes total mitochondrial biogenesis signaling through prolonged AMPK activation and calcium flux. Easy runs are the primary driver of mitochondrial density because the signal accumulates with duration — a 60-minute easy run produces more total PGC-1-alpha upregulation than a 20-minute tempo run, even though the per-minute signal is weaker. This is the main reason 80% of training volume should be easy: it builds the mitochondrial base that every other adaptation depends on.
- **Threshold work** ([tempo runs](#tempo-run), [cruise intervals](#cruise-intervals)): Running at [lactate threshold](#lactate-threshold) produces a stronger per-minute biogenesis signal than easy running because the energy demand is higher (greater AMPK activation) and calcium flux is faster. Threshold work also improves mitochondrial efficiency — the ability of existing mitochondria to oxidize pyruvate faster, clearing it before it spills over to lactate. This is why threshold training raises the threshold: more mitochondria, each working more efficiently.
- **VO2max intervals** ([VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals)): High-intensity intervals produce the most potent per-minute mitochondrial signal through massive AMPK activation and reactive oxygen species generation. They are particularly effective at stimulating biogenesis in fast-twitch (Type II) muscle fibers, which have lower baseline mitochondrial density than slow-twitch fibers and are not recruited during easy running. For a 160 lb runner who also lifts — and therefore has substantial fast-twitch fiber mass — VO2max intervals are especially valuable because they convert those strength-oriented fibers into hybrid fibers with both force-production capacity and aerobic capability.
- **Long runs** ([progressive long runs](#progressive-long-run), [long runs with threshold segments](#long-run-with-threshold-segments)): Long runs deplete glycogen stores, which amplifies the AMPK signal and upregulates fat oxidation enzymes within mitochondria. Glycogen-depleted running ("training low") produces even stronger mitochondrial biogenesis signaling, though it must be used carefully because performance and recovery suffer. The last miles of a long run, when glycogen is running low and the body shifts toward fat oxidation, are when the mitochondrial stimulus is highest — this is one reason long runs should not be cut short.

**Mitochondrial density vs. mitochondrial function.** Density refers to the total volume of mitochondria per unit of muscle — more engines per cell. Function refers to how efficiently each mitochondrion operates — how fast its enzymes process substrates and how tightly coupled its electron transport chain is. Both matter. Early in a training cycle (first 4–8 weeks), density increases rapidly as new mitochondria are built. Over longer periods (months to years), function improves through enzymatic upregulation, cristae remodeling (the inner folds of the membrane where the electron transport chain sits), and mitochondrial network optimization (fusion of smaller mitochondria into larger, more efficient networks). This is why experienced runners with the same VO2max as newer runners often have better endurance — their mitochondria are individually more efficient after years of cumulative adaptation.

**Timelines.** Mitochondrial biogenesis begins within hours of a training session and peaks at 24–48 hours post-exercise. Measurable increases in mitochondrial density appear within 2–4 weeks of consistent training. Mitochondrial density roughly doubles in previously untrained muscle after 6–8 weeks of endurance training. However, mitochondrial adaptations are also lost relatively quickly during detraining — approximately 50% of gains are lost within 2–3 weeks of inactivity, which is why consistency matters more than any single workout. For a runner maintaining 40–55 miles per week, mitochondrial density is at or near its trainable ceiling; further gains come primarily from mitochondrial function improvements and fiber-type-specific development through interval work.

### Lactate Threshold

The intensity at which lactate production exceeds the body's ability to clear it, causing lactate to accumulate in the blood. Below threshold, lactate is produced and cleared in equilibrium — you can sustain this effort indefinitely (limited only by fuel). Above threshold, lactate accumulates progressively and eventually forces a slowdown. For a 1:39 half marathoner, threshold pace is approximately 7:15–7:25/mi. The half marathon is raced at or just below this intensity, making threshold the most performance-relevant physiological marker. Threshold is improved by [tempo runs](#tempo-run) and [cruise intervals](#cruise-intervals), which spend sustained time at the threshold intensity to upregulate lactate-clearing enzymes (monocarboxylate transporters), increase the muscle's buffering capacity, and improve [mitochondrial](#mitochondria) efficiency so that more pyruvate is oxidized aerobically before spilling over to lactate. Threshold can also rise as a secondary effect of [VO2max](#vo2max) improvements — when the ceiling goes up, the percentage of VO2max at which threshold occurs often rises with it.

### VO2max

The maximum rate at which the body can consume oxygen during exercise — the aerobic ceiling. Determined primarily by cardiac output (heart rate x stroke volume), oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood (hemoglobin mass), and peripheral oxygen extraction by muscles. For a 1:39 half marathoner, VO2max is approximately 50–55 mL/kg/min. At 160 lbs (~72.6 kg), the heavier body weight lowers the relative (per-kg) VO2max compared to a lighter runner with the same absolute oxygen consumption — this is one reason heavier runners face a performance ceiling in distance events. VO2max is improved by [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) — running at 95–100% of VO2max for 3–5 minute intervals, which produces the stimulus for increased stroke volume and [mitochondrial density](#mitochondria) (particularly in fast-twitch fibers that easy running does not recruit). VO2max sets the upper limit: if your VO2max supports a 19:30 5K, you cannot run a 18:30 5K without raising it. However, VO2max is highly trainable and responds quickly (4–6 weeks) to targeted interval work.

### Anaerobic System

Produces ATP without oxygen through two pathways: the phosphocreatine system (immediate, lasts ~10 seconds) and glycolysis (lasts ~2 minutes, produces lactate as a byproduct). The anaerobic system fuels the kick at the end of a race, surges to cover a move, and hill sprints. It contributes only 1–5% of energy during a half marathon but determines the outcome when races come down to a finishing sprint. The phosphocreatine system is trained by short, maximal efforts with full recovery ([strides](#strides), [speed intervals](#speed-intervals)). Glycolytic capacity is trained by [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) and race-pace work. Buffering capacity — the ability to tolerate the hydrogen ions produced by glycolysis — improves with [tempo runs](#tempo-run) and [threshold work](#threshold-workouts). At 160 lbs with substantial muscle mass from lifting, the anaerobic system is likely a relative strength — more muscle produces more force during kicks and surges. This is an advantage in tactical races.

## Biomechanics

### Stride Mechanics

Running speed equals cadence (steps per minute) times stride length. Both can be improved, but the mechanisms and tradeoffs differ. **Cadence** should trend toward 175–180 spm for distance running — higher cadence reduces ground contact time, lowers vertical oscillation (wasted energy), decreases braking forces, and reduces injury risk by shortening the lever arm at impact. For a runner with large calves, higher cadence is particularly beneficial because it reduces the time each stride spends in the swing phase, partially offsetting the metabolic cost of heavier distal mass. **Stride length** increases primarily through greater hip extension and ground force at push-off, not by overstriding (landing with the foot far ahead of the center of mass). Overstriding increases braking forces, ground contact time, and impact loading. Focus on "running tall" with a slight forward lean from the ankles, landing with the foot beneath the hips, and driving off the ground rather than reaching forward.

### Running Economy

The oxygen cost of running at a given pace — the running equivalent of fuel efficiency. Two runners with identical [VO2max](#vo2max) will race at different speeds if their economies differ. Economy is determined by biomechanical efficiency (minimizing wasted motion), elastic energy return (how efficiently tendons store and release energy), muscle fiber type composition, and metabolic efficiency. For a runner with large calves, economy is a double-edged factor: the larger [calf-Achilles complex](#calf-achilles-complex) stores and returns more elastic energy per stride (improving economy), but the heavier distal mass increases the energy cost of swinging the legs (worsening economy). Net effect depends on tendon stiffness, stride rate, and speed — at higher speeds where elastic return dominates, large calves are increasingly advantageous. Economy improves through high mileage (years of consistent running refine movement patterns), [strides](#strides) and [speed intervals](#speed-intervals) (neuromuscular efficiency at high speeds), and [hill repeats](#hill-repeats) (specific strength that improves push-off mechanics). Body weight also matters: at 160 lbs, losing even 3–5 lbs of non-functional mass improves economy measurably, but only if the loss is fat, not muscle — muscle loss reduces force production and elastic energy return.

### Gait Cycle

Each running stride consists of two phases: **stance phase** (foot on the ground, ~35–40% of stride time at distance pace) and **swing phase** (foot in the air, ~60–65%). The stance phase includes initial contact (foot strikes the ground), midstance (body passes over the foot), and push-off (foot leaves the ground). The swing phase includes follow-through (leg travels behind), recovery (leg swings forward), and terminal swing (leg prepares for landing). Unlike walking, running includes a **float phase** where both feet are airborne — this is what makes running "running." Ground contact time (GCT) decreases as speed increases: a 7:30 pace has a GCT of roughly 250–270 ms, while a 6:00 pace has ~220–240 ms. Lower GCT at a given pace indicates better elastic energy return and more efficient force application. With a long torso, the body's center of mass is higher, which increases the rotational moment during stance — the core must resist this rotation through anti-rotation bracing on every single stride. This is why trunk stability work (covered in the [lifting program](../lift/)) has direct transfer to running performance for long-torso runners.

## Musculoskeletal

### Calf-Achilles Complex

The [gastrocnemius](../lift/#gastrocnemius), [soleus](../lift/#soleus), and Achilles tendon function as a spring system that stores elastic energy during landing and releases it during push-off — this stretch-shortening cycle is the primary mechanism for locomotion economy. The Achilles tendon returns approximately 35% of the energy of each stride for free, making it the most important elastic structure in running. The soleus absorbs more force than any other muscle during distance running and is the primary endurance muscle of the lower leg. With large calves, the muscle component of this system is overdeveloped relative to typical distance runners. Advantage: more muscle generates more push-off force and stores more elastic energy per stride, benefiting hill running and kick speed. Risk: more muscle means more force transmitted through the Achilles tendon per stride, increasing the risk of [Achilles tendinopathy](#injury-history). Manage this by running easy days genuinely easy, increasing mileage conservatively (no more than 10% per week), and monitoring for mid-tendon morning stiffness — an early warning sign. The calf-Achilles complex is best maintained through eccentric calf raises (straight-knee and bent-knee) from the [lifting program](../lift/#calves).

### Hip Extensors

The [gluteus maximus](../lift/#gluteus-maximus) and [hamstrings](../lift/#hamstring-muscles) drive hip extension during push-off — this is the power phase of each stride and the primary determinant of stride length at any given cadence. The glutes are the largest muscle in the body and produce peak force at full hip extension, which is why powerful hip extension separates fast runners from slow ones. The [gluteus medius](../lift/#gluteus-medius) stabilizes the pelvis during single-leg stance (every stride is a single-leg stance), preventing hip drop and knee valgus — weakness here is a primary risk factor for IT band syndrome, patellofemoral pain, and shin splints. The hamstrings function eccentrically during late swing phase to decelerate the leg before footstrike — this is when hamstring strains occur, particularly during sprinting. For a runner who also lifts, the hip extensors are likely a relative strength. Maintain this with [hip thrusts](../lift/#barbell-hip-thrusts), [Bulgarian split squats](../lift/#bulgarian-split-squats), and [Nordic hamstring curls](../lift/#nordic-hamstring-curls) from the lifting program.

### Quadriceps and Knee Stabilizers

The [quadriceps](../lift/#quadriceps) absorb impact during landing (eccentric knee flexion) and extend the knee during push-off. They do more work during downhill running than uphill, which is why downhill races cause more muscle soreness. The vastus medialis oblique (VMO) is critical for patellar tracking — weakness contributes to runner's knee (patellofemoral pain syndrome), the most common running injury. At 160 lbs, the eccentric demand on the quads during landing is higher than for lighter runners at any given pace. This means greater muscle damage per run and slower recovery — another reason easy days must be genuinely easy. Quad strength from squatting ([back squats](../lift/#barbell-back-squat), [leg extensions](../lift/#leg-extensions)) provides a protective buffer.

### Foot and Ankle

The plantar fascia is a thick band of tissue on the sole of the foot that acts as a spring during the stance phase, storing and returning elastic energy similar to the Achilles tendon. Plantar fasciitis — inflammation of this tissue — is the most common foot injury in runners and is more prevalent in heavier runners due to greater ground reaction forces. The [tibialis anterior](../lift/#tibialis-anterior) dorsiflexes the ankle during swing phase to clear the foot; weakness or fatigue contributes to shin splints. The [tibialis posterior](../lift/#tibialis-posterior) supports the arch during stance; dysfunction leads to flat feet and medial ankle pain. The [peroneals](../lift/#peroneals) stabilize the ankle against inversion sprains. All of these structures are loaded proportionally to body weight, making foot and ankle health a higher priority for a 160 lb runner than for a 130 lb runner. Arch-strengthening exercises (towel curls, single-leg calf raises) and adequate shoe support are non-negotiable.

### Core in Running

The core's job during running is to resist unwanted motion — anti-extension, anti-rotation, and anti-lateral-flexion — on every single stride. The [transversus abdominis](../lift/#transversus-abdominis) braces the trunk, the [obliques](../lift/#external-obliques) resist rotation as the arms and legs drive in opposite directions, and the [quadratus lumborum](../lift/#quadratus-lumborum) resists lateral flexion as body weight shifts from leg to leg. With a long torso, these rotational demands are amplified because the longer lever arm increases the rotational moment the core must counteract. Core fatigue in the second half of a race causes pelvic instability, loss of hip extension power, and deteriorating form — this is often what "hitting the wall" looks like biomechanically. The core work in the [lifting program](../lift/#core) — [suitcase carries](../lift/#suitcase-carries), [ab wheel rollouts](../lift/#ab-wheel-rollouts), [hanging leg raises](../lift/#hanging-leg-raises) — directly transfers to running performance. For a long-torso runner, this transfer is disproportionately valuable.

## Cardiopulmonary

### Heart

Endurance training enlarges the heart's left ventricle, increasing stroke volume — the amount of blood ejected per beat. Higher stroke volume means more oxygen delivered per heartbeat, which lowers resting heart rate and allows higher cardiac output at maximal effort. Resting heart rate is a useful proxy for cardiovascular fitness: well-trained distance runners typically have resting heart rates of 45–55 bpm. **Cardiac drift** — a gradual increase in heart rate during a steady-effort run, even when pace remains constant — is caused by dehydration, rising core temperature, and plasma volume shifts. Drift of 5–10% over 60 minutes is normal; excessive drift indicates dehydration or overheating. For a 160 lb runner, the heart must pump more blood to support the larger body mass, which means cardiac output demands are higher at any given pace — this is another mechanism by which body weight affects distance running performance.

### Lungs and Breathing

Ventilation (liters of air per minute) increases linearly with exercise intensity up to the ventilatory threshold, then rises exponentially as the body attempts to blow off CO2 produced by anaerobic metabolism. At easy pace, breathing is nasal or mixed nasal-oral and unconscious. At threshold pace, breathing becomes rhythmic and deliberate — a 2:2 pattern (two steps inhale, two steps exhale) is common. At VO2max pace, breathing becomes 2:1 or even 1:1. The lungs are rarely the limiting factor in trained runners — ventilation capacity exceeds the body's ability to use oxygen. However, breathing patterns affect running rhythm and core stability: each exhale reduces intra-abdominal pressure, momentarily reducing trunk stiffness. This is why foot strike timing relative to breathing can affect side stitch occurrence. For a long-torso runner, the larger thoracic cavity may offer a slight ventilation advantage — more lung volume — but this is a minor factor compared to cardiac output and peripheral adaptations.

### Blood

**Hemoglobin** is the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen from lungs to muscles. Total hemoglobin mass is the strongest predictor of VO2max and is why blood doping is effective. Endurance training increases hemoglobin mass by 10–15% over months. **Plasma volume** — the liquid portion of blood — expands early in training (within days), which temporarily dilutes hemoglobin concentration even though total hemoglobin mass is unchanged or increasing. This is why hemoglobin levels can appear to drop ("sports anemia") in newly training athletes. **Iron** is essential for hemoglobin synthesis and is the most common nutritional deficiency in distance runners, especially those training at high volumes. Iron is lost through sweat, foot-strike hemolysis (red blood cells destroyed by impact), and GI losses. At 160 lbs, foot-strike hemolysis is higher due to greater impact forces. Monitor ferritin levels (aim for >30 ng/mL, ideally >50); supplement if deficient.

## Stimulus Quality

These factors determine how effectively a workout drives the intended physiological adaptation.

### Specificity

How closely a workout mimics the physiological and biomechanical demands of the goal race. For a half marathon, the most specific workout is a sustained effort at threshold pace for 20–40 minutes — it replicates the intensity, duration type, and metabolic demands of racing. [Tempo runs](#tempo-run) and [long runs with threshold segments](#long-run-with-threshold-segments) rank highest in specificity. Easy runs and speed intervals rank lowest — they develop foundational systems but do not directly simulate race conditions. Specificity should increase as race day approaches: base phase favors general development, peak phase favors race-specific work.

### Time at Target Intensity

The total minutes spent in the intended physiological zone during a workout. A 40-minute [tempo run](#tempo-run) provides 40 minutes at [threshold](#lactate-threshold) intensity. Six [cruise intervals](#cruise-intervals) of 6 minutes each provide 36 minutes at threshold with better fatigue management. Ten minutes of a progression run at threshold provides only 10 minutes of quality despite a longer total session. When comparing workouts that target the same system, prefer the one that maximizes time at the target intensity per unit of [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost). More time at intensity means more adaptation stimulus.

### Aerobic Contribution

The percentage of a workout's stimulus that develops the [aerobic system](#aerobic-system). Easy runs are 100% aerobic. [Tempo runs](#tempo-run) are approximately 95% aerobic. [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) are approximately 90% aerobic. [Speed intervals](#speed-intervals) are approximately 70–80% aerobic. Even "anaerobic" workouts have a substantial aerobic component because the aerobic system provides background energy production during recovery intervals and maintains baseline oxygen consumption. For half marathon training, prioritize workouts with high aerobic contribution — the race itself is 95–99% aerobic.

## Workout Structure

These factors describe how a workout is organized and how that organization affects its role in training.

### Continuous vs. Interval

**Continuous** workouts ([tempo runs](#tempo-run), [steady long runs](#steady-long-run), [general aerobic runs](#general-aerobic-run)) maintain a steady effort without rest breaks. They build sustained effort tolerance, mental toughness, and pacing discipline. **Interval** workouts ([VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals), [cruise intervals](#cruise-intervals), [speed intervals](#speed-intervals)) alternate between work and recovery, allowing more total time at higher intensities with lower [fatigue cost](#fatigue-cost). The recovery periods partially restore phosphocreatine, clear some lactate, and allow the cardiovascular system to remain at high output across more repetitions. Use continuous workouts for race simulation and sustained threshold development. Use interval workouts for VO2max development and when accumulating more volume at intensity than a continuous effort allows.

### Volume vs. Intensity

The fundamental tradeoff in training: more miles at easier paces versus fewer miles at harder paces. Both develop the [aerobic system](#aerobic-system) but through different mechanisms. Volume builds capillary density, mitochondrial density, fat oxidation, and musculoskeletal durability — adaptations that take months to develop but persist for months after detraining. Intensity builds [VO2max](#vo2max), [lactate threshold](#lactate-threshold), and [running economy](#running-economy) — adaptations that develop faster (weeks) but are also lost faster. The 80/20 rule reflects the optimal balance for most distance runners: 80% easy volume for the slow-building adaptations, 20% intensity for the fast-responding ones. Runners who violate this by running too much at moderate intensity ("the gray zone") accumulate fatigue without maximizing either adaptation pathway.

### Terrain

Different surfaces change the training stimulus. **Flat roads** allow precise pace control and are most specific to road racing. **Hills** build strength and [VO2max](#vo2max) with lower impact forces (uphill) but higher eccentric loading (downhill). **Trails** reduce impact forces by 10–20% on soft surfaces, develop proprioception and ankle stability, and provide mental variety, but prevent precise pace control. **Track** allows the most precise interval pacing and is flat, but the constant left turns increase asymmetric loading on the left leg. **Treadmill** eliminates weather and terrain variability, allows precise pace and incline control, and reduces eccentric loading (the belt assists leg return), but slightly alters [biomechanics](#stride-mechanics) — set 1% incline to approximate outdoor effort. At 160 lbs, softer surfaces (trails, grass, treadmill) are beneficial for high-volume weeks and long runs to manage cumulative impact.

## Practical Factors

These factors determine how well a workout fits into the realities of training logistics, recovery, and long-term sustainability.

### Fatigue Cost

The total recovery cost of a workout — how many days of reduced training are needed afterward. [Steady long runs](#steady-long-run) at easy pace have moderate fatigue cost (1 easy day after). [Tempo runs](#tempo-run) have moderate-high fatigue cost (1 easy day after). [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) have high fatigue cost (1–2 easy days after). [Long runs with threshold segments](#long-run-with-threshold-segments) have the highest fatigue cost (2–3 easy days after). At 160 lbs, every workout has a higher fatigue cost than for lighter runners because more mass means more musculoskeletal stress per mile. This is the primary reason heavier runners need to be conservative with weekly quality sessions — two hard days per week plus a long run is typically the maximum sustainable frequency.

### Injury Risk

The probability of a workout causing acute or overuse injury. Risk increases with speed (higher ground reaction forces), mileage (cumulative load), surface hardness (less shock absorption), and fatigue (degraded biomechanics). The highest-risk workouts are long, fast runs on hard surfaces — exactly the combination a long run with threshold segments represents. The lowest-risk workouts are short, easy runs on soft surfaces. At 160 lbs, impact forces per stride are approximately 15–20% higher than for a 135 lb runner at the same pace. This risk is cumulative: each individual stride is fine, but the summation over weeks and months increases stress fracture, tendinopathy, and plantar fascia risk. Mitigate by choosing softer surfaces for long and easy runs, limiting speed work frequency, and monitoring weekly mileage increases.

### Weather and Conditions

Heat and humidity are the most performance-limiting environmental factors. Heat raises core temperature, increases cardiac drift, reduces blood available to muscles (diverted to skin for cooling), and dramatically increases perceived effort. Paces should be adjusted: add 15–30 seconds per mile for every 10 degrees above 60 degrees F. Humidity compounds the effect by reducing evaporative cooling. Cold weather is less problematic physiologically but increases warm-up time and can stiffen muscles and tendons. Altitude reduces oxygen partial pressure and degrades performance immediately (2–3% per 1,000 feet above 5,000 feet) but triggers [EPO production](#blood) and hemoglobin mass increases after 2–3 weeks of exposure. For a 160 lb runner, heat management is more challenging because the larger body produces more metabolic heat while having a lower surface-area-to-mass ratio for cooling.

### Equipment and Location

Consistent training requires accessible, repeatable conditions. Track access enables precise interval pacing but is not available to everyone. A GPS watch allows pace monitoring on any terrain. A treadmill provides weather-proof, cushioned running with precise pace and incline control — useful for [tempo runs](#tempo-run) and [threshold work](#threshold-workouts) in bad weather. Shoes with adequate cushioning and appropriate support matter more at higher body weights — replace shoes every 300–400 miles (heavier runners compress midsoles faster). For large calves, ensure shoes have adequate room in the upper to avoid compression. The best workout is the one you can execute consistently.

## Programming Context

These factors relate to how a workout fits within the broader training plan rather than its standalone qualities.

### Periodization Phase

Training is organized into phases that shift the emphasis between volume and intensity as a race approaches. **Base phase** (8–12 weeks): high volume, low intensity — build the aerobic engine with [easy runs](#general-aerobic-run), [steady long runs](#steady-long-run), and one weekly [tempo run](#tempo-run) or [fartlek](#fartlek). Strides maintain speed. **Build phase** (6–8 weeks): moderate volume, increasing intensity — add [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) and [progressive long runs](#progressive-long-run). Two quality sessions per week. **Peak phase** (3–4 weeks): slightly reduced volume, highest specificity — include [race-pace intervals](#race-pace-intervals), [long runs with threshold segments](#long-run-with-threshold-segments), and [tempo runs](#tempo-run) at or faster than current threshold. **Taper** (10–14 days): reduce volume by 40–60% while maintaining some intensity to stay sharp. Eliminate long runs over 10 miles, keep one short tempo or a few strides.

### Recovery Compatibility

A workout must be compatible with the rest of the training week. A [VO2max interval](#vo2max-intervals) session and a [long run with threshold segments](#long-run-with-threshold-segments) in the same week may create more fatigue than the runner can recover from. Consider the total weekly stress budget: at 160 lbs, the budget is smaller than for lighter runners. The standard structure — one interval day, one threshold day, one long run — works because each stresses different systems and allows recovery between. Avoid two high-impact sessions on consecutive days. Place recovery runs or rest days between quality sessions. If lifting the same week, schedule leg-heavy lifting as far from quality runs as possible.

### Race Proximity

The closer the goal race, the more specific training should become and the less appropriate experimentation is. During base phase (12+ weeks out), try new workouts, test paces, and build volume. During build phase (6–8 weeks out), refine the workout mix and settle into race-specific patterns. During peak phase (3–4 weeks out), do your key race-specific sessions and nothing novel. During taper (10–14 days out), reduce volume, maintain some sharpness, and rest. Never introduce a new workout type within 3 weeks of a goal race.

## Individual Factors

These factors vary from person to person and make workout selection inherently individual.

### Anthropometry

Body weight is the single most important anthropometric factor in distance running because every stride must lift and propel the entire body. At 160 lbs, approximately 15–20% more energy is required per mile compared to a 135 lb runner — this is primarily why lighter runners dominate distance events. The long torso raises the center of gravity, increasing rotational demands on the core and making [trunk stability](#core-in-running) more important. Large calves increase distal mass, raising the metabolic cost of leg swing but also enhancing elastic energy return through the [calf-Achilles complex](#calf-achilles-complex) — the net effect on [running economy](#running-economy) depends on speed, cadence, and tendon stiffness. These anthropometric factors cannot be changed (short of weight loss), so training should work with them: higher cadence to offset calf mass, core work to manage the long torso, and conservative mileage progression to manage the impact cost of body weight.

### Injury History

Previous injuries change which workouts are appropriate and how quickly volume and intensity can increase. Common running injuries and their implications: **Achilles tendinopathy** (particularly relevant with large calves) — reduce hill work and speed intervals, emphasize eccentric calf strengthening, avoid sudden mileage increases. **Plantar fasciitis** (more common at higher body weights) — use cushioned shoes, avoid barefoot running, do arch-strengthening exercises. **IT band syndrome** — strengthen [glute medius](../lift/#gluteus-medius), reduce downhill running, check shoe wear patterns. **Shin splints** — strengthen [tibialis anterior](../lift/#tibialis-anterior), increase cadence to reduce impact, avoid hard surfaces. **Stress fractures** (higher risk at 160 lbs) — ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D, increase mileage no more than 10% per week, use softer surfaces. Pain is a workout selection signal: if a workout hurts, modify it (slower pace, softer surface, shorter duration) before abandoning it entirely.

### Training Age

Years of consistent running determine how much volume and intensity the body can tolerate. A runner with 2 years of experience responds differently to 50 miles per week than a runner with 10 years. With a 1:39 half marathon, this runner likely has 2–5 years of structured training. At this stage, the priority shifts from building base aerobic fitness (which is already solid) to refining the training mix: more threshold work, targeted VO2max development, and strategic use of [race-pace intervals](#race-pace-intervals). Long-term (5+ years), the training stimulus required to improve becomes increasingly specific and the margin for error narrows — [periodization](#periodization-phase) and [recovery management](#recovery-compatibility) become more important than raw volume.

### Goals

The goal race determines the training emphasis. **Sub-1:35 half marathon** (next target): emphasize [threshold work](#threshold-workouts) — the half marathon is a threshold race. Tempo runs and cruise intervals are the priority quality sessions, supplemented by weekly [VO2max intervals](#vo2max-intervals) to raise the ceiling. **Sub-1:30 half marathon** (stretch goal): requires both threshold improvement and VO2max development, plus improved [running economy](#running-economy) through strides, speed work, and higher overall mileage. **Marathon** (if attempted): shift to higher volume (55–65 miles/week), longer long runs (18–20 miles), and marathon-pace work; threshold and VO2max work remain but long run becomes the centerpiece. **5K/10K racing**: shift to more interval work, less threshold volume, shorter long runs; develop top-end speed. **General fitness**: maintain 30–40 miles/week with one quality session and one long run; minimize injury risk and prioritize consistency over performance.
